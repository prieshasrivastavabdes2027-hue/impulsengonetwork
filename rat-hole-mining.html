The Problem of Rat-Hole Mining

A Hidden Crisis Underground

Rat-hole mining remains one of the most dangerous and invisible forms of labour exploitation in Northeast India. In this method, workers — including young children — crawl into narrow vertical pits that lead to cramped, poorly ventilated tunnels to extract coal by hand. These shafts can be less than a meter high, forcing workers to crouch and drag sacks of coal through the dark.

Many children trafficked for this work are unaware of the life-threatening risks: collapsing walls, toxic air, and long-term respiratory damage. The mines operate with minimal safety measures, yet families in poverty often feel they have no other choice but to send children to work.

“Thousands of children have been trapped in this hidden economy for years — invisible, unheard, and unprotected.”

How Children Are Pulled Into the System

Over the past decade, investigations revealed a cycle of trafficking where children from Nepal, Bangladesh, and neighbouring Indian states were recruited with false promises of safe work. Instead, they entered hazardous mines where accidents are frequent and often fatal.

INGON’s Intervention

Impulse NGO Network led the first major studies on rat-hole mining, documenting cases of child labour, trafficking pathways, and mine-linked exploitation. Through rescue operations, collaborations with law enforcement, and survivor rehabilitation, INGON has helped bring national and international attention to an issue once hidden from the world.
